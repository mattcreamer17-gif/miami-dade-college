01

Where things stand

Miami Dade College cut the ribbon on the Kendall AI Innovation Hub in March. 7,000 square feet. Three AI facilities across campus. NAAIC carrying NSF, Google.org, Intel, and Microsoft money. A bachelor's in Applied AI that was the first in Florida. Microsoft reporting a 15-point lift on pass rates from the Copilot rollout.

The college has moved faster on AI than almost any two-year institution in the country. The next step is the harder one. Making the methodology consistent enough to travel to every NAAIC partner college, and durable enough to survive the next accreditation review.

02

The 60/30/10 frame

A useful way to look at any college's AI stack. About 60 percent of what looks like an AI problem is actually traditional software or database work that never needed an LLM. Another 30 percent is rule-based logic with a clean deterministic answer. The remaining 10 percent is genuinely probabilistic and belongs on a model.

Most institutions spend budget putting AI on the first 60 percent and then wonder why the results are uneven. Applied to MDC specifically, the advising workflow, the Banner-to-Canvas-to-Copilot data path, and the NAAIC curriculum review process each have a clear answer to that 60/30/10 question once you slow down and look. That is the work. Deciding which layer each piece belongs on, and then designing the orchestration so every layer is inspectable.

How it maps on a campus

60% Traditional code. Banner joins, Canvas reports, enrollment pipelines.
30% Rule-based logic. Financial aid packaging, prerequisite checks, degree audit.
10% Genuine AI. Advising conversation, curriculum adaptation, faculty workflow design.

The paper underneath

Interpretable Context Methodology is the framework. Submitted to ACM TiiS. It treats agent context as a layered filesystem, which is the structure NAAIC will need if the curriculum is to travel across partner colleges without drifting.

Repository (MIT license): github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM-

A secondary asset for the governance conversation is the Ethics Engine, a psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs, documented at arxiv.org/abs/2510.11742 with code at github.com/RinDig/AuditEngine.

Eduba partners with NLP Logix for work that sits below the orchestration layer. NLP Logix has been in machine learning since 2011 and runs over 150 data scientists.
